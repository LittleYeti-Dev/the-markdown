1. Executive Summary

Both RFIs are approved for execution. This document serves as the dual-project governance plan for the Hybrid AI Development Stack ("Robo Stack") and the Non Sequitur Content Refinery Platform ("Content Ops"), running in parallel under Yeti Knowledge Systems.

Both projects were scored across feasibility, revenue potential, and strategic value. Content Ops scores higher on near-term feasibility (3.6 vs 2.6) while Robo Stack carries the stronger revenue ceiling. The projects are synergistic — Content Ops showcases and markets the thinking behind Robo Stack, while Robo Stack can eventually power Content Ops automation.

A key lesson from the Markdown project informs this plan: process and workflow design must happen up front, not be discovered in the final sprint. Accordingly, every epic in both backlogs now begins with a process design and best-of-breed evaluation gate before any build work starts. Yeti is the sole stakeholder and primary touchpoint for all decisions — every sprint ends with a structured review checkpoint.

2. Lessons Learned & Design Principles

Lesson from the Markdown Project

The project workflow wasn't fully defined until the final sprint. This caused rework, ambiguity during execution, and a product that was shaped more by discovery than by design. The corrective principle for both RFIs:

Design the process before building the product. Every epic starts with "how will this work?" before "let's build it."

Governing Principles for Both RFIs

Process-First Design: Each epic begins with a workflow/process design story before any implementation stories. The process is documented in Markdown, reviewed by Yeti, and locked before build work begins. No building without a designed process.
Best-of-Breed Evaluation Gates: Every significant tool, platform, or technology choice goes through a formal evaluation gate (marked yellow in backlogs). The evaluation produces a decision document comparing options against criteria relevant to both projects. No tool is adopted on assumption — it earns its place.
Requirements Captured from Capabilities: As each capability is built and tested, requirements are captured and fed back into the plan. The backlog is a living document — stories refine as real-world capability reveals what's needed next. Each touchpoint includes a "requirements harvest" where new learnings shape upcoming sprints.
Single Stakeholder Touchpoints: Yeti is the sole decision-maker and quality gate for both projects. Every sprint ends with a structured touchpoint that includes: demo of work completed, review of evaluation gate decisions, requirements harvest from what was learned, and explicit go/no-go for the next sprint. Nothing advances without Yeti sign-off.
Tight Plan → Tight Product: The RFI waves (Discovery → Design → Convergence) are respected. Each wave produces tighter artifacts than the last. By the end, both projects deliver not just a working product but a documented, repeatable operational process that can be handed to someone else.
Future Delegation Path (Content Ops): While Yeti is the primary touchpoint now, Content Ops is designed to eventually delegate evaluation and editorial roles. The system captures process decisions explicitly so that when blog evaluation, social distribution, or editorial review is handed off, the new person inherits a designed process — not tribal knowledge.

5. Team Role Personas

Robo Stack Team

Platform Architect / Lead Engineer
80–100% allocation
Owns Kubernetes configuration, Terraform modules, AWS infrastructure, and CI/CD pipeline design. Makes technology selection decisions across the AI agent layer. Responsible for security architecture and IAM policy. This is the project driver — without this role filled and active, the project stalls.
AI Integration Specialist
40–60% allocation
Evaluates and integrates AI coding agents (Copilot, TabbyML, CodeWhisperer). Builds the orchestration layer that routes between agents. Develops benchmarks comparing agent output quality. Can be the same person as Platform Architect in a lean team, but the work is distinct.
DevSecOps Engineer
30–50% allocation
Implements dependency scanning, SAST/DAST integration, encrypted comms, and centralized logging. Owns the security hardening epic. Reviews all infrastructure-as-code for compliance. In a solo operation, this merges with Platform Architect but the responsibilities remain.
Solutions Consultant (Phase 4+)
As needed post-launch
Translates the platform into client-facing consulting packages. Creates pricing models, SOWs, and demo scripts. Manages client engagements for stack deployment, security audits, and architecture consulting. This role activates only after the demo app is live.

Content Ops Team

Content Systems Architect
60–80% allocation
Designs the capture-to-publish workflow in Notion/Obsidian. Builds automation rules, stage gates, and the AI prompt library. Defines the thematic arc templates and production cadence. This is the operational brain of the platform — the person who decides how the system works so the creator doesn't have to.
Creator / Editorial Lead
50–70% allocation
The primary voice. Captures ideas, works the editorial pipeline, writes long-form content, records podcast/video. Once the system is built, this person operates within it rather than designing it. The whole point of Content Ops is to free this role from process decisions.
Distribution & Social Operator
20–40% allocation
Transforms blog content into social posts, newsletters, and short-form clips. Manages scheduling tools (Buffer, etc.), monitors engagement, and feeds analytics back into the thematic arc planning. Can be AI-assisted in Phase 2+.
Multimedia Producer (Phase 3)
As needed — Phase 3 only
Handles podcast editing, video post-production, thumbnail creation, and publishing to YouTube/podcast platforms. Activates only when the content pipeline is mature enough to support multimedia output consistently.

6. Operational Standards — DevOps Baseline

Both projects should adhere to a unified DevOps operational standard, anchored in the Markdown-first methodology already in use. The following standards apply to both projects and form the governance framework for the YKS DevOps group:

Documentation Standard: All project documentation lives in Markdown. Architecture decisions, sprint retrospectives, runbooks, and status reports are authored in .md and version-controlled in Git. No documentation lives exclusively in Notion/Confluence without a Markdown mirror.
Source Control Standard: Git-based repositories with branch protection on main. Feature branches follow naming convention: feature/{project}-{epic}-{description}. All merges require at minimum a self-review checklist.
Infrastructure-as-Code Standard: All infrastructure provisioned via Terraform (Robo Stack) or declarative configuration files (Content Ops tooling). No manual environment setup. Reproducibility is a hard requirement.
CI/CD Standard: Every project maintains an automated pipeline. For Robo Stack: lint → test → security scan → build → deploy. For Content Ops: validate → stage → schedule → publish. Pipelines are defined in YAML and stored in the repo.
Security Standard: Least-privilege access on all systems. API keys in environment variables or secrets managers — never in code. Dependency scanning on every build. Quarterly manual security review.
Sprint & Delivery Standard: 2-week sprints. Each sprint ends with a demo-able increment. Retrospective notes in Markdown. Backlog maintained in a Kanban tool with labels matching epic IDs from this document.
Naming & Taxonomy Standard: Projects use codenames (Robo Stack, Content Ops). Epics numbered E1–En. Stories numbered S{epic}.{sequence}. All artifacts reference these IDs for traceability.
Quality Gate Standard: No story marked "Done" without acceptance criteria met. No epic closed without a demo artifact. No project advances past a phase gate without stakeholder sign-off documented in Markdown.

8. Sprint 0 — Foundational Decisions (Gates Both Projects)

EV0.1: GitLab vs GitHub — Source Control & DevOps Platform

Current state: GitLab. Under consideration: GitHub, primarily for its AI capabilities (Copilot integration, Copilot Workspace, AI-powered code review, Actions marketplace). This is a high-impact decision that affects CI/CD, project management, documentation hosting, and AI tooling for both projects.

9. Execution Plan — Robo Stack Leads, Content Ops Follows

Two Separate Projects, Shared Sprint 0, Staggered Execution

Both RFIs execute as separate projects with independent backlogs, repos, and wikis. Sprint 0 is shared — it resolves foundational decisions that gate both. After Sprint 0, Robo Stack leads as the primary execution track. Content Ops follows once Robo Stack's infrastructure patterns are established and can inform Content Ops tooling choices.

Execution sequence:

Sprint 0 (Week 0–1) — SHARED: Resolve GitLab vs GitHub, project management tooling, wiki standard, documentation conventions. Initialize repos and wikis for both projects. Both projects blocked until Sprint 0 complete.

Weeks 1–6 (Sprints 1–3) — ROBO STACK PRIMARY: Robo Stack sprints Local Dev, AI Agents, and CI/CD (Epics 1–3). All best-of-breed evaluations and process designs documented in the wiki as they happen. Content Ops in background planning only — refining its backlog based on patterns emerging from Robo Stack.

Weeks 7–10 (Sprints 4–5) — ROBO STACK + CONTENT OPS BEGINS: Robo Stack pushes into AWS Infra and Security Hardening (Epics 4–5). Content Ops starts Sprint 1 — Capture system and platform evaluation. Two parallel tracks, each with their own Yeti touchpoints.

Weeks 11–14 (Sprints 6–7) — BOTH ACTIVE: Robo Stack builds demo app and consulting packages. Content Ops builds editorial pipeline and cadence system. Both converge at Week 14 for a full portfolio review.

Why Robo Stack first: It produces the harder infrastructure decisions, the DevOps patterns, and the CI/CD muscle that Content Ops can inherit. Running Robo Stack first means Content Ops doesn't have to solve platform and process questions that Robo Stack already answered.

Wiki from Day 1: Every decision doc, evaluation gate result, process design, and retrospective goes into the project wiki the day it's created — not backfilled later. The wiki is the project's memory.
